# Federal Civil Rights Litigation in CPS-Origin Cases

This skill encodes the framework for federal civil rights litigation arising from CPS / child welfare misconduct.

## The statutes

### 42 USC §1983 — Civil action for deprivation of rights

Elements:
1. Conduct by a person
2. Acting under color of state law
3. Depriving plaintiff of a right secured by federal law (Constitution or federal statute)
4. Causation

Applies to: state and local officials, including CPS workers, agency supervisors, GALs (when state actors), contracted service providers when functioning under state authority.

Does NOT apply to: federal officials (Bivens may), purely private actors (joint action / state nexus exceptions exist).

### 42 USC §1985(3) — Conspiracy to interfere with civil rights

Elements:
1. Conspiracy
2. For the purpose of depriving equal protection
3. Class-based, invidiously discriminatory animus (most circuits)
4. Act in furtherance
5. Injury

Limitations:
- Class-based animus requirement narrows applicability
- Intracorporate conspiracy doctrine: in many circuits, employees of a single entity cannot conspire among themselves
- Personal stake exception narrows the intracorporate bar

### 42 USC §1986 — Action for neglect to prevent

Liability for those who knew of an impending §1985 conspiracy and could have prevented it but did not.

## Constitutional rights commonly invoked in CPS cases

### 14th Amendment Procedural Due Process

- Notice and meaningful opportunity to be heard (Mathews v. Eldridge balancing)
- Right to confrontation in dependency proceedings (varies; some courts apply Crawford-style analysis)
- Right to a competent, prepared adjudicator
- Evidence fabrication violates due process (Devereaux v. Abbey, 263 F.3d 1070 (9th Cir. 2001))
- Discovery obstruction can violate due process if it materially impairs the ability to defend

### 14th Amendment Substantive Due Process — Family Integrity

- Fundamental right to family integrity (Stanley v. Illinois, 405 U.S. 645 (1972); Santosky v. Kramer, 455 U.S. 745 (1982); Troxel v. Granville, 530 U.S. 57 (2000))
- "Shock the conscience" standard for executive-action violations (County of Sacramento v. Lewis, 523 U.S. 833 (1998))
- Standard varies by deliberate indifference vs. intent depending on context

### 4th Amendment

- Seizure of child without warrant requires either (a) exigent circumstances or (b) court order (Tenenbaum v. Williams, 193 F.3d 581 (2d Cir. 1999); Wallis v. Spencer, 202 F.3d 1126 (9th Cir. 2000))
- Reasonable suspicion / probable cause standards apply
- Continued seizure based on fabricated evidence violates 4A

### 1st Amendment Retaliation

Elements (post-Nieves v. Bartlett, 139 S. Ct. 1715 (2019)):
1. Protected activity
2. Adverse action
3. Causal connection (temporal proximity often sufficient circumstantially)

Cases: Hartman v. Moore, 547 U.S. 250 (2006); Mt. Healthy City School Dist. Bd. of Ed. v. Doyle, 429 U.S. 274 (1977)

### Equal Protection

- Race, religion, national origin, disability — strict or heightened scrutiny
- Class-of-one (Village of Willowbrook v. Olech, 528 U.S. 562 (2000)) — intentionally treated differently from others similarly situated without rational basis

### Access to Courts

- Christopher v. Harbury, 536 U.S. 403 (2002)
- Frustration of viable underlying claim

## Monell municipal liability

Monell v. Dept. of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978)

A municipality is liable under §1983 only if the constitutional violation was caused by:

1. An official policy (written policy, ordinance, regulation, decision)
2. A widespread custom or practice (so widespread it constitutes the "moving force")
3. A decision by a final policymaker (with final authority under state law)
4. Failure to train, supervise, or discipline showing deliberate indifference (City of Canton v. Harris, 489 U.S. 378 (1989); Connick v. Thompson, 563 U.S. 51 (2011))

CPS-specific Monell theories:

- Custom of using unauthenticated forensic evidence (toxicology, etc.)
- Custom of ignoring statutory discovery obligations
- Title IV-E financial incentives creating systemic pressure for removal/placement decisions
- Inadequate training on evidence authentication
- Inadequate complaint and discipline processes for caseworker misconduct
- Contracting structure with service providers (drug testing labs) without oversight
- Coordination patterns with affiliated agencies (DA, GAL panel, etc.)

## Qualified immunity

Two-prong analysis (Saucier v. Katz, Pearson v. Callahan):

1. Did the conduct violate a constitutional right?
2. Was that right "clearly established" at the time?

"Clearly established": existing precedent must have placed the question beyond debate — generally requiring Supreme Court holding or robust consensus of circuit authority with closely analogous facts.

EXCEPTION: Hope v. Pelzer, 536 U.S. 730 (2002) — conduct that is so obviously unconstitutional that no specific precedent is needed.

Defeating qualified immunity requires:
- Specific identification of the right at the appropriate level of specificity
- Cases that gave fair notice
- Factual congruence between cited cases and instant conduct
- For obvious violations: framing as Hope-style

## Abstention doctrines

### Younger v. Harris, 401 U.S. 37 (1971)

Federal courts must abstain from interfering with ongoing state proceedings if:
1. State proceedings are ongoing
2. They implicate important state interests
3. They provide adequate opportunity to raise federal claims

Post-Sprint Communications v. Jacobs, 571 U.S. 69 (2013), narrowed to:
a. State criminal prosecutions
b. State civil enforcement proceedings akin to criminal
c. State civil proceedings involving orders uniquely in furtherance of state courts' judicial function

CHIPS / dependency cases — circuit-by-circuit and posture-by-posture; argue for narrow Sprint reading.

EXCEPTIONS:
1. Bad faith / harassment
2. Patently invalid statute
3. Extraordinary circumstances showing irreparable injury

STRATEGIES:
- Younger doesn't bar damages claims — stay rather than dismiss
- Argue bad-faith exception with documented pattern of retaliatory action
- File while state proceedings continue, accept stay, preserve federal claims

### Rooker-Feldman doctrine

Applies only to:
1. State-court losers
2. Complaining of injuries caused by state-court judgments
3. Rendered before federal proceedings commenced
4. Inviting federal court review of those judgments

Narrowed by Exxon Mobil Corp. v. Saudi Basic Industries, 544 U.S. 280 (2005).

Independent federal claims (not seeking review of state judgment) are NOT barred — Skinner v. Switzer, 562 U.S. 521 (2011).

STRATEGY: Plead independent injuries from defendants' conduct, not "the court got it wrong." Seek prospective relief and damages, not reversal of state judgment.

### Heck v. Humphrey, 512 U.S. 477 (1994)

Bars §1983 claims that would "necessarily imply the invalidity" of a criminal conviction not yet invalidated.

Most CPS-origin claims do NOT trigger Heck because:
- No criminal conviction in CPS proceeding itself
- Focus on process/conduct, not factual innocence
- Civil dependency orders are not "convictions" under Heck

### Domestic relations exception

Federal court abstention from divorce, alimony, child custody decrees (Ankenbrandt v. Richards, 504 U.S. 689 (1992)).

Limited to those three categories. CPS / dependency civil rights claims typically not within the exception. Do NOT seek custody orders from federal court.

### Eleventh Amendment

States and state agencies cannot be sued for damages in federal court without consent.

- Individual capacity: damages OK
- Official capacity: prospective injunctive/declaratory relief OK under Ex Parte Young, 209 U.S. 123 (1908)
- Official capacity damages: barred
- Counties and their employees: usually NOT immune (Lake Country Estates v. Tahoe Reg'l Plan. Agency, 440 U.S. 391 (1979))

## State notice-of-claim laws

Federal §1983 claims are NOT subject to state notice-of-claim statutes (Felder v. Casey, 487 U.S. 131 (1988)).

State-law claims joined under supplemental jurisdiction (28 USC §1367) DO require state notice-of-claim compliance.

## Statute of limitations

§1983 borrows the state personal-injury statute of limitations.

Common periods:
- Wisconsin: 3 years (Wis. Stat. §893.54)
- Minnesota: 6 years (Minn. Stat. §541.05)
- California: 2 years
- Always verify current state law.

Accrual: when plaintiff knew or should have known of the injury.

## Damages

### Compensatory

Available for actual injury including:
- Economic losses (income, costs)
- Non-economic (emotional distress, loss of family integrity, loss of consortium with child)
- Future damages

Carey v. Piphus, 435 U.S. 247 (1978) — actual injury required (rejected pure presumed damages for procedural violations).

### Punitive

Smith v. Wade, 461 U.S. 30 (1983) — available against individuals (not municipalities) for evil motive or reckless indifference.

### Attorney's fees

42 USC §1988 — prevailing party may recover reasonable attorney's fees.

Pro se litigants generally cannot recover their own fees (Kay v. Ehrler, 499 U.S. 432 (1991)). But can recover costs, expert fees in some circumstances, and may be eligible for limited counsel fees if attorneys assisted.

## Pleading standards (Twombly/Iqbal)

Federal claims must contain "factual content that allows the court to draw the reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged" (Ashcroft v. Iqbal, 556 U.S. 662 (2009)).

For CPS-origin §1983:
- Identify each defendant's specific conduct
- Tie conduct to specific constitutional violations
- Allege causation
- Plead Monell elements with specificity (not boilerplate)
- Address qualified immunity proactively where possible
- For conspiracy: plead at minimum: meeting of the minds, parallel conduct + plus factors

## TRO / Preliminary Injunction

Winter v. NRDC, 555 U.S. 7 (2008) — four factors:
1. Likelihood of success on the merits
2. Irreparable harm absent relief
3. Balance of equities
4. Public interest

In CPS-origin federal cases:
- Irreparable harm from loss of family integrity (Stanley line)
- Likelihood from documented constitutional violations
- Balance: defendants have no legitimate interest in unconstitutional conduct
- Public interest in constitutional compliance and family integrity

Be careful about scope — avoid Rooker-Feldman-implicating relief (don't ask federal court to reverse state court order).

## Verification

Federal complaints can be verified under 28 USC §1746. Required for some TRO motions. Adds credibility and signals confidence.

## When to consult counsel

Federal civil rights litigation is complex. Even with this skill, the user should seek counsel for:
- Defendant identification and capacity decisions
- Strategic decisions about timing and forum
- Discovery planning
- Settlement negotiation
- Trial preparation

Pro bono and reduced-fee options:
- ACLU state chapter
- Civil rights organizations (Institute for Justice, Center for Constitutional Rights)
- Law school clinics
- Local bar association referral services
- Civil rights bar associations

This skill produces drafts and analysis. Counsel produces strategy and judgment.
