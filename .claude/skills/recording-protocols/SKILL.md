# Recording Protocols

Recordings can be the most powerful evidence in a CPS case — party-opponent admissions, witness statements, agency conduct. But recording laws vary, and unlawful recording can be criminal and is inadmissible.

## The two regimes

### One-party consent jurisdictions (majority of US states)

Only one party to the conversation needs to consent. If you are a party to the conversation, you can record without notifying others. This includes:

- Phone calls you participate in
- In-person conversations you participate in
- Meetings you attend

### Two-party (all-party) consent jurisdictions

All parties to the conversation must consent. Recording without consent is illegal — often criminal.

Two-party consent states (as of writing — VERIFY current law in your state):

- California (Cal. Penal Code §632)
- Connecticut
- Delaware
- Florida (Fla. Stat. §934.03)
- Hawaii (mixed)
- Illinois (mixed; 720 ILCS 5/14-2 has nuances post-People v. Clark)
- Maryland (Md. Code §10-402)
- Massachusetts (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 272 §99)
- Michigan (mixed)
- Montana (Mont. Code §45-8-213)
- Nevada (Nev. Rev. Stat. §200.620)
- New Hampshire
- Oregon (Or. Rev. Stat. §165.540)
- Pennsylvania (18 Pa. Cons. Stat. §5704)
- Washington (Wash. Rev. Code §9.73.030)
- Vermont (court-created, somewhat mixed)

CRITICAL: Verify current law for your state. Laws change. Some states have nuanced rules (e.g., expectation of privacy considerations).

### Federal law

18 USC §2511 — one-party consent. But state law applies when state law is more protective.

## Recommended best practices regardless of jurisdiction

1. **Disclose when possible.** Even in one-party states, telling people you're recording often makes recordings more usable.

2. **Get explicit consent for important meetings.** At the start of a meeting or call: "I'm recording this conversation. Is that okay with everyone?" If anyone objects, stop. Otherwise their continuation is consent.

3. **Disclose at the start of every recording session.** Even if you've previously disclosed.

4. **Don't record in spaces with reasonable expectation of privacy.** Bathrooms, bedrooms (if you're not present), homes you don't have permission to enter.

5. **Don't record children secretly.** Even with one-party consent, recording your child without their other parent's knowledge can be weaponized against you in family court.

## What to record

### YES — high-value targets

- Phone calls and in-person meetings with caseworkers
- Phone calls with agency supervisors
- Meetings with GAL
- Meetings with service providers about your case
- Visits (visit-day interactions with supervisors, foster parents, agency staff)
- Court hearings (where permitted — most allow with notice; some require permission)

### NO — high-risk

- Conversations with your attorney (privileged; recording could waive)
- Conversations where the other party has expectation of privacy
- Conversations with your child where the recording could be characterized as covert
- Court hearings without prior permission in jurisdictions that require it

## Court hearing recording

Each court has its own rules:

- Federal courts: generally NO recording without specific permission (FRCrP 53 prohibits photographing or broadcasting; civil rules vary)
- State trial courts: vary widely; many allow audio recording with notice; many do not
- Appellate oral argument: many allow / are themselves recorded
- Always check the court's standing order or contact the clerk's office in advance

Always: have a court reporter or get the official transcript ordered. Your recording is supplemental, not primary.

## Technical practices

### Devices

- Phone voice memo apps (iOS Voice Memos, Android Recorder)
- Dedicated voice recorders (Olympus, Sony, Zoom) — preferred for important meetings
- Body cameras (legal where allowed)

### Quality

- High-bitrate setting (preserves voice detail)
- Test before important meetings
- Battery / storage check before each session
- Backup recording device for critical meetings

### File handling

After each recording:
1. Transfer to your evidence vault (`05-evidence/recordings/`)
2. Rename with date and subject: `2025-08-06_DHS_meeting.m4a`
3. Hash with SHA-256
4. Log in `_evidence-hashes.md`
5. Make a transcript (services: Otter.ai, Rev.com, Trint; or manual)
6. Save transcript alongside recording
7. Do NOT modify the original recording

### Transcripts

For court use:
- Verbatim
- Time-stamped where possible
- Speaker-identified
- Notation for unclear / inaudible
- Certified transcripts for evidentiary use (court reporter or transcription service that certifies)

Your AI-assisted transcript is for analysis, not for filing without verification.

## Admissibility considerations

For a recording to come in as evidence:

1. **Lawfulness** — recorded in compliance with applicable law (one-party / two-party)
2. **Authentication** (FRE 901)
   - Witness who can identify voices
   - Distinctive characteristics
   - Process or system showing accuracy
3. **Best evidence rule** (FRE 1002) — original or duplicate
4. **Hearsay analysis** — statements may be:
   - Not hearsay (party-opponent admissions FRE 801(d)(2); prior statement of declarant FRE 801(d)(1))
   - Hearsay with exception (excited utterance, present sense impression, business records)
   - Inadmissible hearsay (excluded)
5. **Chain of custody** — preserved unmodified from recording to court

## What NOT to do

- Don't record secretly in two-party states
- Don't record privileged communications with your own attorney
- Don't record in places with reasonable expectation of privacy
- Don't share recordings publicly during pending litigation without counsel's advice
- Don't post recordings to social media (waiver, defamation risk, contempt risk)
- Don't edit recordings (any edit destroys evidentiary value)
- Don't lose the original (the original is your custody-of-record)

## Visit recording specifically

In supervised visit settings:

- Many agencies have written policies prohibiting parents from recording during visits
- Some such policies are legally questionable but enforcement is real
- Recording in violation may be used against you ("uncooperative parent" framing)
- Strategy: ask in writing for permission to record; document the response; comply with the answer; build the record about the policy

Court orders may explicitly permit or prohibit recording — read carefully.

## Disclosure obligations in discovery

Recordings in your possession that relate to the case are typically discoverable. If opposing party asks for "all recordings" and you have them, you must produce them (or properly object).

Don't make recordings you'd be afraid to produce.

## The principle

Record lawfully. Preserve completely. Don't edit. Verify what's discoverable before refusing production. The recording's value comes from its authenticity and admissibility; protect both.
