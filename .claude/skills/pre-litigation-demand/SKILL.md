# Pre-Litigation Demand Letter

The pre-litigation demand letter is a strategic instrument. Drafted well, it creates settlement leverage, satisfies notice requirements, triggers preservation duties, and sets the foundation for future litigation. Drafted poorly, it educates opposing counsel and locks you into untenable positions.

## The strategic posture: FACT-HEAVY, ARGUMENT-LIGHT

This is the most important principle. The demand letter:
- Recites facts in detail
- Identifies generic categories of claims
- Does NOT detail legal theory
- Does NOT cite specific cases or doctrines
- Does NOT explain how the elements are met
- Does NOT preview the trial strategy

Why: Every word you write becomes a roadmap for the defense. The more you say about your theory, the more time they have to prepare for it. Marshal the facts; let them figure out the theory.

## Goals (in order of importance)

1. **Trigger litigation hold obligations** — once the recipient has notice of potential litigation, they have a duty to preserve evidence
2. **Satisfy any state notice-of-claim requirements** — though not required for §1983 (Felder v. Casey), state-law claims often have notice requirements
3. **Create settlement window** — some defendants will pay to avoid litigation if shown the case is real
4. **Establish your sophistication** — opposing counsel reads this and decides whether you're a credible threat
5. **Preserve statutes of limitations** — tolling effects in some jurisdictions
6. **Build the record** — your statements here will be referenced by the court

## Goals to AVOID

- Detailing legal theory
- Identifying specific cases you'll rely on
- Specifying damages too precisely (anchors low)
- Making demands so high they signal unreasonableness
- Naming witnesses you don't want them to contact
- Attaching evidence (preserves discovery leverage)
- Personal attacks
- Emotional rhetoric

## Structure

### 1. Header

```
SENT VIA CERTIFIED MAIL, RETURN RECEIPT REQUESTED
                  AND VIA EMAIL TO: [counsel email]

[Date]

[Officials by name and title]
[Agency name]
[Agency address]

CC:
[Risk Management]
[Insurance carrier]
[Outside counsel if any]
[Agency director / county counsel]
```

### 2. Subject line

Specific enough to identify, generic enough not to give away strategy:

"Notice of Potential Claims Arising from [County] DHS Case [NN-JC-NNNNNN]"

NOT: "Section 1983 fabrication and First Amendment retaliation claims"

### 3. Introduction (one paragraph)

Identify yourself, your child, the case, and that this letter constitutes notice of potential claims. Note that the letter triggers litigation preservation obligations.

### 4. Statement of facts (3-5 pages)

Numbered paragraphs. Chronological. Each fact:
- Sourced (general reference; do not name witnesses you don't want contacted)
- Dated
- Specific
- Free of legal characterization

Show breadth and depth of evidence WITHOUT revealing the analytical framework.

Example of right tone:
"On August 6, 2025, plaintiff and counsel for DHS engaged in a recorded conference. During that conference, DHS personnel made specific statements regarding the absence of documentation supporting key allegations in the underlying CHIPS proceeding. These statements have been preserved."

NOT: "On August 6, 2025, DHS personnel made party-opponent admissions under FRE 801(d)(2) that prove the agency's case lacks foundation under [case citation]..."

### 5. Categorical statement of potential claims

Generic categories. No detail.

"The conduct described above implicates federal constitutional claims under 42 U.S.C. §1983, including but not limited to procedural due process, substantive due process, First Amendment, and Fourth Amendment claims; conspiracy claims under 42 U.S.C. §1985; entity liability claims under Monell; and state-law claims sounding in tort and contract."

That's enough. They know the case is real.

### 6. Litigation hold notice

This is critical and must be specific. Identify categories of evidence to preserve:

"You and your agents are hereby on notice to preserve, and to instruct your employees, contractors, and agents to preserve, all evidence relating to the matters described above, including but not limited to:

- All written, electronic, and recorded communications (email, text, voicemail, instant messaging, internal collaboration platforms, telephone records) involving plaintiff, plaintiff's child, plaintiff's counsel, or the underlying CHIPS proceeding
- All case files, records, and notes maintained by DHS personnel including [named personnel by title not name where possible]
- All metadata associated with electronic documents produced in the CHIPS proceeding
- All audit logs and access records for case management systems
- All contracts and communications with third-party service providers including drug testing vendors, evaluators, and supervised visitation providers
- All policies, training materials, and supervisor reviews relating to the conduct described
- All records reflecting federal funding (Title IV-E) associated with placement decisions
- All complaints, grievances, and disciplinary records concerning [personnel by title]

The duty to preserve attaches as of receipt of this letter. Any failure to preserve, including through routine deletion policies that should be suspended, may result in spoliation sanctions."

### 7. Statutory notices

If state-law claims are contemplated and the state has notice-of-claim requirements, reference compliance:

"To the extent any of the contemplated claims fall within [STATE]'s Notice of Claim statute, [STATE STATUTE], this letter is intended to satisfy that requirement. Plaintiff intends to comply with all applicable pre-suit requirements."

### 8. Settlement invitation

Brief. Specific deadline. No specific demand unless strategically chosen.

"Plaintiff is willing to discuss resolution of these matters before litigation. Please direct any communications regarding resolution to plaintiff at [contact]. If we have not heard from you within thirty (30) days of receipt of this letter, plaintiff will proceed accordingly."

### 9. No-waiver language

"Nothing in this letter waives any right, claim, or remedy, all of which are expressly preserved. The omission of any specific claim, theory, or fact from this letter is not a waiver."

### 10. Signature

Plaintiff signature. If counsel involved, counsel signs. Pro se litigants sign and identify themselves as pro se.

## Specific demand or no demand?

Two schools of thought:

**Specific demand**: Sets the high anchor. "Plaintiff demands $X to resolve these claims without litigation." Pros: anchors high; signals seriousness. Cons: may seem unreasonable; locks in number; may push toward early posturing.

**No specific demand**: "Plaintiff is willing to discuss resolution." Pros: preserves negotiation flexibility; doesn't signal desperation. Cons: signals you may not know the value; loses anchoring.

Default: no specific demand in first letter. Make demand in follow-up after they respond, or in mediation.

## Recipients

Don't send only to the most junior official. Send to:
- Named individual defendants who will be sued
- Their supervisors
- Agency director
- County counsel / city attorney
- State risk management (if applicable)
- Outside counsel (if known to be retained)
- Insurance carrier (if known)

The broader the distribution, the more pressure on the defendants and the harder for any one of them to ignore.

## After sending

Calendar:
- 30 days for response
- 35 days: follow-up if no response
- 45 days: file complaint if no resolution

Document:
- Date sent
- Certified mail tracking
- Email send and delivery confirmations
- Any responses (preserve all)

If they ignore the letter, that itself becomes evidence in the federal case (bad faith, refusal to engage in good-faith resolution).

## Common mistakes

- Detailing legal theory (educates them)
- Attaching evidence (loses leverage)
- Sounding angry (loses credibility)
- Making demands too high (signals unreasonableness)
- Making demands too low (anchors low)
- Failing to include litigation hold language
- Sending only to one recipient
- Failing to copy insurance / risk management
- Setting deadline too short (signals desperation)
- Setting deadline too long (signals weakness)

## The principle

The defense reads your letter and decides: is this person a real threat or can we wait them out? The letter must communicate: "I have facts. I have evidence. I have time. I will file if you don't engage."

Without revealing how you will win.
