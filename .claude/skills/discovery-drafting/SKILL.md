# Discovery Drafting

Discovery is where cases are won and lost. The agency has more institutional resources but less institutional memory. Pro se parents who discover well force admissions, expose contradictions, and build the record for everything that follows.

## Discovery in dependency proceedings vs. federal civil cases

**Dependency / CPS proceedings**: Discovery is more limited than full civil discovery. Each state has its own rules. Common features:
- Right of access to the case file
- Right to witness lists
- Right to expert reports
- Statutory open records / public records access
- Often no formal interrogatories or RFAs (varies)

**Federal civil rights cases**: Full Federal Rules of Civil Procedure discovery (FRCP 26-37):
- Initial disclosures (FRCP 26(a))
- Interrogatories (FRCP 33; max 25)
- Requests for production (FRCP 34)
- Requests for admission (FRCP 36)
- Depositions (FRCP 30)
- Subpoenas (FRCP 45)

This skill applies to both, with adjustment for the rules in the active forum.

## Principles

### 1. Every discovery request has a purpose

Before drafting, identify: what specific factual proposition or impeachment opportunity does this request serve?

Bad: "Provide all documents about this case." (Overbroad; will be objected to.)

Good: "Produce all written communications between Caseworker Johnson and any third party between [date] and [date] concerning the toxicology testing of [plaintiff]." (Specific, time-bounded, purpose-driven.)

### 2. Anticipate objections in the drafting

Common objections:
- **Overbroad** — narrow with time, person, and topic limits
- **Unduly burdensome** — proportionate to needs of case
- **Vague** — define terms in a definitions section
- **Privileged** — exclude privileged communications by language
- **Not reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence** — tie to relevance
- **Compound** — one fact per request

Draft to defeat each.

### 3. Force the trilemma

Discovery should be designed to put opposing parties into trilemmas:
- Produce → confirms your theory
- Refuse → discovery sanctions
- Object → must commit to a basis you can challenge

## Interrogatories

Format:

```
INTERROGATORY NO. 1: Identify each person who provided any information used in preparing the [report dated X], including for each: (a) name, (b) role, (c) date of communication, (d) substance of information provided, and (e) any written record of the communication.
```

Number sequentially. State definitions section at top:

```
DEFINITIONS

1. "You" or "Your" refers to [party] and any agents, employees, contractors, or representatives acting on their behalf.
2. "Document" has the meaning given in FRCP 34(a)(1)(A) and includes electronic data.
3. "Communicate" or "Communication" means any transmission of information, whether oral, written, electronic, or otherwise.
4. "Identify" when used with respect to a person means: name, current or last-known address, current or last-known employer, and role relevant to the subject matter.
5. "Identify" when used with respect to a document means: title, type, date, author, recipient, current custodian, and Bates number if produced.
6. "The Case" means [specific case identification].
7. "The Period" means [date] through [date] unless otherwise specified.
```

Then interrogatories — typically 10-25, prioritized:

- BACKGROUND: training, role, authority, supervisor
- POLICIES: identify policies governing the conduct at issue
- COMMUNICATIONS: identify communications by category
- ACTIONS: what they did, when, with whom, basis
- KNOWLEDGE: what they knew, when, from whom
- WITNESSES: identify witnesses to specific events
- EXPERT BASIS: for each expert opinion, the basis
- PRIOR INCIDENTS: same actor in prior similar situations (pattern)

## Requests for production

Format:

```
REQUEST NO. 1: All documents, communications, recordings, and electronically stored information relating to [specific subject matter], including but not limited to [enumerated categories], from [date] through [date].
```

Format requirements:
- Native files preferred with metadata intact
- Format as kept in usual course of business (FRCP 34(b)(2)(E))
- Specific Bates labeling for production tracking

Categories to cover in CPS-origin cases:

1. Complete case file in native format
2. All communications (email, text, internal messaging) involving [parties] regarding [child / case]
3. All policies, procedures, manuals, training materials governing the conduct
4. All contracts with third-party service providers
5. All investigative materials including notes, drafts, recordings, photographs
6. All calendars, schedules, time entries for personnel
7. All financial records / billing for the case
8. All prior complaints against named personnel
9. All audit / supervisor review materials
10. For each piece of evidence the agency intends to introduce, all source / native records

## Requests for admission

Format:

```
REQUEST FOR ADMISSION NO. 1: Admit that the document attached as Exhibit A is a true and correct copy of the toxicology report dated [date] purporting to test specimen from [plaintiff].
```

Strategy: RFAs lock in undisputed facts and force formal denials of disputed facts. Failure to respond admits the fact (FRCP 36(a)(3)). Denying something true triggers fee-shifting (FRCP 37(c)(2)).

Categories of RFAs:

- AUTHENTICATION: "Admit that Exhibit X is a true and correct copy of..."
- AGENCY STATEMENTS: "Admit that on [date], [official] stated [quote]..."
- AGENCY ACTIONS: "Admit that [agency] did not [action] between [dates]..."
- POLICY/PROCEDURE: "Admit that [policy] provides [provision]..."
- STATUTORY ELEMENTS: "Admit that [statute] requires [requirement]..."
- UNDISPUTED FACTS: "Admit that plaintiff [did X / attended Y]..."
- ABSENCE OF EVIDENCE: "Admit that [agency] possesses no documentation of [claim]..."

Keep each RFA to one discrete fact, phrased for yes/no, anchored to verifiable evidence.

## Subpoenas

For non-parties (labs, hospitals, schools, third-party witnesses):

```
SUBPOENA DUCES TECUM TO [ENTITY]

YOU ARE COMMANDED to produce the following documents:

1. [Specific request 1]
2. [Specific request 2]
...

at [location] by [date and time].

A custodian of records affidavit / declaration may be provided in lieu of personal appearance, attesting to authenticity and business-records foundation under FRE 902(11) / state equivalent.
```

Requirements:
- Issued by court clerk or attorney of record (varies by jurisdiction)
- Served under FRCP 45 or state equivalent
- Notice to all parties
- Recipient's privacy interests considered (HIPAA, FERPA, third-party privacy)

For testimony subpoenas, also include date, time, location, and witness fee tender.

## Definitions and instructions sections

Always include at the top of any discovery set:

- Definitions (terms used throughout)
- Instructions (how to respond, how to identify documents, how to claim privilege)
- Time period (default or specific)
- Format of production (native, PDF, etc.)
- Privilege log requirements (if any documents withheld)

Privilege log requirement:

"If any document responsive to these requests is withheld on the basis of privilege, identify with respect to each such document: (a) the type of document, (b) the date, (c) the author and recipients, (d) the general subject matter, (e) the privilege asserted, and (f) the factual basis for the privilege."

## Drafting for the agency-witness deposition

Discovery requests prime the deposition. Build:

- Interrogatories that force specific factual commitments
- RFAs that lock authentication and policy facts
- RFPs that produce the documents you'll use as exhibits in the deposition
- Subpoenas to non-parties for their files (medical, school, lab) before the deposition

By the time the deposition starts, you should know what the witness will be forced to admit.

## Common drafting mistakes

- Boilerplate "any and all" language (objections magnet)
- Compound requests (one fact per request)
- Vague time periods
- Asking the witness to draw legal conclusions
- Asking for "all" without specifying
- Failing to define key terms
- Not coordinating across instruments (interrogatories should set up RFAs which should set up depositions)

## Responding to discovery (the other direction)

If opposing party serves discovery on the user:
- Calendar the response deadline
- Object specifically and timely
- Produce what is required
- Withhold privileged material with privilege log
- Move for protective order if discovery is harassing or overbroad
- Don't volunteer; respond to the specific request

See Prompt 47 (Discovery Objection Response) and Prompt 34 (Motion for Protective Order).
